# DLBSR_CLASS_SUPPORT_POSITIVE_CRITERION_NOTE

## Support-positive criterion for DLBSR-class in the first compatibility pass

**Date:** 2026-04-07  
**Status:** internal criterion note

---

## 1. Purpose
This note defines the first-pass **support-positive criterion** for:

> **DLBSR-class = Downstream Local Bridge-Support Response class**

The purpose is to answer the next narrowing question:

> **What exactly must be true for a local or mesoscopic structure to count as DLBSR-positive rather than DLBSR-negative?**

This is necessary because a binary class is only useful if the positive side is defined:
- early enough,
- narrowly enough,
- and without collapsing into the later-stabilization boundary.

This is a criterion note, not a result note.

---

## 2. Why this criterion must be explicit
DLBSR-class has already been fixed as:
- the first downstream outcome proxy
- in binary form
- later than Candidate A and Candidate B
- narrower than full readability

But without an explicit support-positive criterion, the class remains too soft.

Then several risks appear:
- positive gets assigned too loosely
- positive gets assigned post hoc
- positive becomes a vague synonym for “looks promising”
- or positive collapses into raw rigidity-like persistence

So the support-positive criterion must be declared before first-pass comparison.

---

## 3. Working conceptual meaning of support-positive
The intended meaning should be:

> **A structure counts as DLBSR-positive only if a narrow later local bridge-support response has materialized strongly enough that support is no longer merely implied, but operationally present at a local downstream level.**

A shorter version is:

> **DLBSR-positive means later local support is materially present, not just hinted at.**

That is the intended meaning.

---

## 4. What the support-positive criterion should do
The criterion should do four things at once:

### 4.1 Stay downstream of the candidates
It must be later than Candidate A and Candidate B.

### 4.2 Stay narrower than full readability
It must not jump to morphology, geometry, or broad readable structure.

### 4.3 Stay distinct from the stabilization boundary
It must not reduce to:
- “rigidity is high, therefore positive”

### 4.4 Be strong enough to avoid trivial positivity
It must not be so weak that almost any slight local response counts as support-positive.

This balance is the point of the criterion.

---

## 5. Recommended first-pass criterion shape
For the first pass, the support-positive criterion should be:

> **A pre-declared minimum local downstream bridge-support response threshold crossed by a narrow support-related indicator, with positivity assigned only when the response is clearly above minimal-noise / minimal-hint level.**

In plainer terms:

- not just “some signal exists”
- but “enough local downstream support response exists to count as actually present”

That is the right first-pass shape.

---

## 6. Preferred operational interpretation
The cleanest first-pass interpretation is:

### DLBSR-positive
Assign **DLBSR-positive** only if:
- the chosen narrow downstream support indicator is above a pre-declared support-positive threshold,
- and the excess is strong enough that it counts as actual local downstream support presence rather than a weak fluctuation.

### DLBSR-negative
Assign **DLBSR-negative** if:
- the chosen narrow downstream support indicator does not cross that threshold,
- or the response remains too weak / too marginal to count as materially present.

This is the recommended first-pass binary rule.

---

## 7. What “materially present” should mean
For the first pass, “materially present” should be read conservatively.

It should mean:
- not merely nonzero
- not merely descriptively visible
- not merely slightly elevated
- but sufficiently strong to count as a real narrow downstream support-positive state

So the criterion must reject:
- tiny responses
- ambiguous responses
- weak near-noise responses

This is important because the first pass is a triage block, not a maximally sensitive detector.

---

## 8. What the criterion must not become
The support-positive criterion must **not** become:

### 8.1 A full readability criterion
It must not require later morphology or broad readable structure.

### 8.2 A rigidity clone
It must not simply say:
- rigidity high = DLBSR-positive

That would collapse the target into the upper boundary.

### 8.3 A weak “any signal counts” rule
It must not turn positivity into a trivial label.

### 8.4 A post hoc adjustable threshold
The threshold must not be loosened mid-pass to rescue a candidate.

These must all be avoided.

---

## 9. Recommended threshold philosophy
The threshold should be chosen with the following philosophy:

### Conservative rather than permissive
A first-pass support-positive class should be a little harder to earn than too easy.

Why?
Because false positives here are more damaging than false negatives.

If positivity is too easy:
- Candidate A and B may both appear better than they are
- and the downstream target becomes soft

A conservative threshold makes the first pass cleaner.

---

## 10. Recommended first-pass decision rule
A good generic decision rule for the first pass is:

> **Assign DLBSR-positive only when the chosen narrow downstream support indicator exceeds a pre-declared support threshold clearly enough to count as support-present rather than support-possible. Otherwise assign DLBSR-negative.**

This sentence captures the intended first-pass criterion.

A shorter version is:

> **Positive = support-present, not merely support-possible.**

That is the key distinction.

---

## 11. Relationship to overlap-only baseline
The support-positive criterion must remain later and stronger than overlap-only.

That means:
- raw relational presence is not enough
- overlap by itself must never imply DLBSR-positive

So the criterion must force a real gap between:
- “relation is there”
and
- “later local bridge-support is materially present”

That gap is important for the whole compatibility comparison.

---

## 12. Relationship to later-stabilization boundary
The support-positive criterion must also remain distinct from the upper boundary.

That means:
- DLBSR-positive should not require full achieved persistence
- but also should not be a disguised rigidity threshold

So the criterion should sit:
- above overlap-only
- below or narrower than broad stabilization-side persistence logic
- and clearly before broad readability claims

That is the intended location.

---

## 13. Main risks of a bad support-positive criterion
### Risk 1 — Too permissive
Too many cases become DLBSR-positive.

### Risk 2 — Too rigid
Almost nothing becomes positive, making the target uselessly harsh.

### Risk 3 — Too close to rigidity
Positivity becomes almost the same as crossing the stabilization boundary.

### Risk 4 — Too vague
Different readers interpret “positive” differently.

### Risk 5 — Post hoc redefinition
The threshold gets adjusted after seeing candidate behavior.

These risks must be controlled.

---

## 14. Recommended first-pass discipline
For the first pass, the support-positive criterion must be:

- pre-declared
- binary
- conservative
- explicitly written into the first-pass comparison record
- identical for Candidate A and Candidate B
- and not changed mid-pass

This is mandatory.

---

## 15. Safe summary sentence
A useful internal sentence is:

> DLBSR-positive should be assigned only when a narrow later local bridge-support indicator is strong enough to count as support materially present, not merely descriptively nonzero and not merely support-possible.

A shorter version is:

> **DLBSR-positive = later local support materially present.**

Both are good steering lines.

---

## 16. Bottom line
The first-pass support-positive criterion for DLBSR-class should be:

- narrow
- binary
- conservative
- later than the compatibility candidates
- stronger than overlap-only
- and distinct from the later-stabilization boundary

In working first-pass language:

> **DLBSR-positive is assigned only when later local bridge-support is materially present, not merely hinted at.**

That is the current best first-pass criterion.
