# COMPATIBILITY_CANDIDATE_B_EARLY_REMOVAL_FRAGILITY_NOTE

## Candidate B: early removal fragility as a first compatibility-layer candidate

**Date:** 2026-04-07  
**Status:** internal candidate note  
**Candidate ID:** ERF-B-01

---

## 1. Purpose
This note defines the second concrete Tier-1 compatibility candidate:

> **Early removal fragility**

The purpose is not to claim that this candidate already captures the compatibility layer correctly.

The purpose is narrower:

- to define one concrete fragility-side candidate,
- to place it as early as possible in the mechanism chain,
- and to test whether structural sensitivity can reveal pre-stabilization supportability better than overlap alone.

This is a candidate-definition note, not a result note.

---

## 2. One-sentence meaning
This candidate is intended to capture whether certain local relations or relation-groups already behave as disproportionately support-critical before full stabilization is achieved, and therefore may indicate pre-stabilization supportability structure.

---

## 3. Why this candidate is worth testing
Early removal fragility is worth testing because it asks a different but complementary question from Candidate A.

Candidate A asks:
- where does local structure already look internally coherent?

Candidate B asks:
- where does a small local weakening or removal already reveal unusually strong support dependence?

This is important because compatibility may show up not only as:
- local agreement,
but also as
- early structural vulnerability

That is, some local relation patterns may matter more than their mere presence suggests.

---

## 4. Structural role in the mechanism chain
The intended role of Candidate B is:

- **after** generic overlap
- **before** later stabilization
- **before** morphology / readability

Working mechanism position:

`generic overlap -> early support fragility pattern -> later stabilization possibility -> later form / readability`

This candidate is therefore not meant to describe:
- already-achieved bridge survival,
but rather:
- which local relations appear structurally critical before the bridge has fully “spoken for itself”

That is the intended niche.

---

## 5. Earliest computation point
### Intended earliest computation point
Candidate B should be computed at a stage where:
- local relation structures already exist
- but later bridge-support success is not yet being directly read back into the candidate

That means:
- before later survival labels are used
- before downstream morphology/readability outcomes are used
- and ideally before the bridge is treated as fully stabilized

### Timing judgment
**Target status:** borderline but potentially still pre-stabilization

### Main timing danger
Candidate B is especially vulnerable to becoming too late.

If the removal analysis is performed only on:
- already strongly stabilized bridge structure
- or late-stage graph success objects

then it is no longer compatibility-side fragility.
It becomes:
- a disguised stabilization or survival analysis

This is the largest current risk of Candidate B.

---

## 6. Required inputs
Candidate B should ideally use:

- local relation set or early relational neighborhood
- local support context
- a defined removal or weakening operation
- and a narrow downstream support-response measure

It should **not** require:
- final survival labels as defining inputs
- late morphology/readability measures
- already accepted rigidity-side success as a built-in ingredient

This is essential if Candidate B is to remain compatibility-relevant.

---

## 7. Provisional candidate logic
The intended logic is:

> If removing or weakening a local relation causes disproportionate early damage to downstream support behavior, then that local relation may have been structurally important in a way that generic overlap magnitude alone does not capture.

So the distinction is:

- overlap = relation is present
- early removal fragility = relation appears structurally support-critical

The idea is not that fragility itself is the whole mechanism.
The idea is that it may reveal an early support structure that later stabilization makes more visible.

---

## 8. Provisional procedural definition
A fully fixed formula is not claimed yet.
But the candidate should already be procedurally defined enough for first-pass work.

### Procedural sketch
For each local relation or local relation-group:

1. define the pre-stabilization local structure
2. compute baseline local or downstream support proxy
3. remove or weaken the selected local relation
4. recompute the support-related proxy
5. define fragility as the change induced by that removal

### Minimal formal template
A generic placeholder form could be:

`ERF = baseline_support - perturbed_support`

or more explicitly:

`ERF = Delta S_local`

where:
- `S_local` is a narrow local or downstream support-related measure
- `Delta` quantifies sensitivity under removal/weakening

A larger positive value would then indicate greater fragility / structural dependence.

This is only a working formal shell.

---

## 9. Why this is not just overlap
Candidate B is **not** supposed to be just overlap because overlap magnitude says only:
- that a relation is present
- or that it is strong in a descriptive sense

Early removal fragility instead asks:
- whether the relation is disproportionately support-critical under controlled local weakening

So the intended distinction is:

- overlap = presence / strength
- early removal fragility = structural support-dependence under perturbation

If Candidate B reduces to “stronger overlap disappears and therefore causes larger change,” it has failed to go beyond overlap-only.

---

## 10. Why this is not just stabilization
This is the most important section for Candidate B.

Candidate B is **not** supposed to be later stabilization because it should not simply report:
- which relations were important in an already stabilized bridge
- or which removals damage a fully achieved support regime after the fact

Instead, it must remain:
- an early structural sensitivity signal
- not a retrospective success analysis

If Candidate B works only when applied to already-late stable structure, then it is not compatibility-side fragility.
It is:
- disguised stabilization leakage

This is the central anti-tautology constraint for Candidate B.

---

## 11. Expected directional meaning
### Higher value means
Greater early structural fragility / dependence.
In the intended reading, a higher value means:
- the local relation is more support-critical than overlap-only would suggest
- and may therefore be part of a compatibility-relevant support scaffold

### Lower value means
Less early structural dependence.
In the intended reading, a lower value means:
- the local relation is less support-critical
- or its importance is not distinguishable from generic presence

If the directional meaning becomes ambiguous under implementation, Candidate B weakens.

---

## 12. Main risks
### Main tautology risk
Candidate B may collapse into later stabilization if the removal analysis is performed too late in the pipeline.

### Main overlap-redundancy risk
Candidate B may simply rediscover that large overlaps matter more when removed.

### Main threshold/artifact risk
Fragility may depend strongly on where the local structure is cut or how support is measured.

### Main interpretability risk
A large removal effect may reflect brute centrality or structural exposure rather than genuine compatibility-side supportability.

### Main current risk sentence
The main current risk of Candidate B is that it may look like an early compatibility signal while actually measuring only how much damage can be done to an already late-stage support structure.

---

## 13. Baseline comparison plan
Candidate B should be compared explicitly against:

### Overlap-only baseline
A direct overlap magnitude or overlap-derived baseline.

### Later stabilization proxy
A clearly downstream support or persistence indicator, most naturally:
- `simple_rigidity_surrogate`

### Optional trivial control proxy
A generic removal-on-density or removal-on-magnitude control.

### What would count as success
Candidate B should count as interesting only if it:
- beats overlap-only meaningfully
- remains earlier than later stabilization logic
- and shows interpretable structural dependence rather than brute late-stage damage

### What would count as failure
Candidate B fails if it:
- adds no value beyond overlap
- works only when defined on already stabilized structure
- or is too dependent on arbitrary removal settings
