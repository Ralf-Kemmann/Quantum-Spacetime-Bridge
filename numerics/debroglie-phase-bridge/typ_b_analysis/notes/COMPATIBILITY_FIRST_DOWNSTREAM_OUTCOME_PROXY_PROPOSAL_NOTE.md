# COMPATIBILITY_FIRST_DOWNSTREAM_OUTCOME_PROXY_PROPOSAL_NOTE

## Concrete proposal for the first downstream outcome proxy in the compatibility first pass

**Date:** 2026-04-07  
**Status:** internal proxy proposal note

---

## 1. Purpose
This note fixes a first concrete proposal for the downstream outcome proxy used in the first compatibility-candidate pass.

Its purpose is to answer the question:

> **What is the first narrow later outcome against which Candidate A and Candidate B should be judged?**

The proxy proposed here is intentionally narrow and operational.

It is **not**:
- full readability,
- not morphology,
- not ontology,
- and not identical to the later-stabilization boundary.

It is only a first downstream target.

---

## 2. Proposed proxy name
### Recommended name
**Downstream Local Bridge-Support Response (DLBSR)**

### Short label
**DLBSR**

This is the proposed first downstream outcome proxy.

---

## 3. Why this name is appropriate
The name is chosen to force the right scale and role.

### “Downstream”
The proxy sits later than Candidate A and Candidate B.

### “Local”
The proxy remains narrow and does not jump immediately to global morphology or broad readability.

### “Bridge-Support”
The proxy stays close to the mechanism gap that compatibility work is actually about.

### “Response”
The proxy is not meant to be the whole stabilized bridge itself, but a later measurable support-related effect.

This makes the proxy strong enough for comparison, but still narrow enough for first-pass discipline.

---

## 4. Intended role of DLBSR
DLBSR should function as:

- a later bridge-support-related outcome,
- downstream of Candidate A and Candidate B,
- but narrower than full stabilization or readability,
- and distinct from the rigidity-side boundary.

Its role is to answer:

> **Does an early compatibility candidate relate meaningfully to a later local bridge-support response, without merely becoming a disguised stabilization signal?**

That is the correct role.

---

## 5. What DLBSR is supposed to measure
DLBSR is intended to measure a narrow local or mesoscopic support-related downstream response such as:

- whether a local bridge-support pattern remains viable enough to count as support-positive,
- whether a local support-response rises above a defined minimal support threshold,
- or whether a local downstream bridge-support class is reached or not reached.

The key point is:
- it should be later than the candidates,
- but still narrower than full bridge morphology/readability.

---

## 6. Working conceptual definition
The working conceptual definition should be:

> **DLBSR = a narrow later local bridge-support-related response indicator that tells whether a local or mesoscopic structure has reached a defined downstream support-positive state.**

A shorter version is:

> **DLBSR asks whether later local bridge-support actually materializes strongly enough to register as a downstream support-positive response.**

That is the intended meaning.

---

## 7. Why DLBSR is a better first target than full readability
DLBSR is preferable to full readability because the first compatibility pass should remain close to the mechanism gap.

If the target were too broad, such as:
- later morphology,
- readability,
- or geometry-like structure,

then weak early candidates could be overinterpreted through very indirect downstream effects.

DLBSR is better because it remains:

- later than the candidates,
- support-side,
- narrow,
- and operationally discussable.

That is exactly what the first pass needs.

---

## 8. Why DLBSR is not identical to the stabilization boundary
This distinction must remain explicit.

### Later-stabilization boundary
The upper boundary asks:
- has the candidate collapsed into already-achieved persistence or stabilization?

Current preferred marker:
- `simple_rigidity_surrogate`

### DLBSR
The downstream outcome proxy asks:
- does the candidate relate to a later local bridge-support response outcome?

So the difference is:

- **boundary** = anti-tautology ceiling
- **DLBSR** = downstream comparison target

These two can be related, but they must not be treated as the same thing.

---

## 9. Recommended operational shape of DLBSR
The first operational version of DLBSR should preferably be a simple local downstream support classification or response score.

### Recommended first-pass form
One of the following narrow forms is preferred:

- **DLBSR-class:** local downstream support-positive vs support-negative classification
- **DLBSR-score:** local downstream support response scalar with a clearly interpretable threshold
- **DLBSR-margin:** distance from a defined local downstream support threshold

For the very first pass, the cleanest option is probably:

> **DLBSR-class = a binary or ternary local downstream support-response class**

This keeps the first pass simpler.

---

## 10. Recommended first-pass interpretation
For Candidate A and Candidate B, the first-pass question should be:

> **Does the candidate help predict or separate DLBSR more meaningfully than overlap-only, while remaining earlier than rigidity-side stabilization?**

This makes DLBSR the first actual downstream target of the compatibility comparison.

That is the intended first-pass role.

---

## 11. What DLBSR must not become
DLBSR must not drift into:

- full morphology proxy
- full readability surrogate
- broad “interestingness” outcome
- or a relabeled rigidity measure

If that happens, the first pass loses its narrow mechanistic discipline.

So DLBSR must remain:
- local,
- support-related,
- downstream,
- and narrow.

---

## 12. Main risks of DLBSR
### Risk 1 — Too close to rigidity
If DLBSR is defined too directly from persistence-style support success, it may become too similar to the stabilization boundary.

### Risk 2 — Too broad
If DLBSR includes too much bridge-shape or readability structure, it becomes too late for the first pass.

### Risk 3 — Too vague
If DLBSR is not defined tightly enough, every candidate trend may be narratable as “relevant.”

### Risk 4 — Too threshold-fragile
If DLBSR depends on one brittle support threshold only, it may produce unstable first-pass judgments.

These must be watched explicitly.

---

## 13. Recommended immediate next definition step
The next narrowing step should now be:

> **fix whether DLBSR will be used in the first pass as a class, score, or margin**

Recommended current order of preference:

1. **DLBSR-class**
2. **DLBSR-score**
3. **DLBSR-margin**

For the first pass, DLBSR-class is probably the cleanest start.

---

## 14. Safe summary sentence
A useful internal sentence is:

> The first downstream outcome proxy should be fixed as DLBSR — Downstream Local Bridge-Support Response — because it is later than Candidate A and Candidate B, narrower than full readability, and distinct from the rigidity-side boundary.

A shorter version is:

> **DLBSR = later support response, not full bridge readability and not rigidity itself.**

Both are good steering lines.

---

## 15. Bottom line
The proposed concrete first downstream outcome proxy is:

> **DLBSR = Downstream Local Bridge-Support Response**

Its role is to serve as the first narrow later outcome against which Candidate A and Candidate B are judged.

It is intended to be:

- later than the candidates,
- narrower than full readability,
- distinct from the stabilization boundary,
- and close enough to bridge-support behavior to remain mechanistically meaningful.

That is the current best concrete proposal for the first downstream outcome proxy.
