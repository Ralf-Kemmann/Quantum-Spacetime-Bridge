# BRIDGE_CARRIER_SYNTHESIS_NOTE — M.3.9x
## Physical synthesis note on likely bridge carriers and the current status of the FSW–AO transition picture

**Date:** 2026-04-04  
**Project status:** Internal physical synthesis note  
**Scope:** Consolidated reading of the current robust findings from the Type-B / transfer / holdout workflow

---

## 1. Purpose

This note is intended to prevent the project from losing sight of its physical target while the methodological hardening work continues.

The central question is no longer only whether some pattern survives internal tests. The more important question is now:

> **What do the currently robust findings physically suggest about the bridge between FSW-like and AO-like regimes, and which quantities are plausible bridge carriers rather than merely diagnostic support variables?**

This note therefore aims to:
- summarize the current physical reading,
- distinguish likely carrier variables from methodological scaffolding,
- and sketch the emerging geometry of the bridge in feature space.

---

## 2. Current physical status of the bridge

At the present stage, the bridge is **not yet proven** as a full physical mechanism. But it is also no longer well described as a mere artifact candidate.

The current robust evidence supports a more specific intermediate reading:

> The bridge is most plausibly a **structured transition zone** between regimes, carried by multiple relational order variables and visible as a partially transportable pattern, rather than as a pointwise identity or a single-marker effect.

This reading is supported by the following broad observations:

- the signal survived hardened controls,
- it survived O1/O2/O3 selection tightening,
- it survived boundary and fine scans,
- it survived a first project-near H2 holdout at least in weakened but non-failed form,
- and under reduced H2 diagnostics, the structural signal remained stable even when the inherited full-pattern gate did not fire.

This combination strongly suggests that the bridge is:
- **not trivially local**,
- **not fully regime-internal**,
- and **not adequately described by one scalar marker alone**.

---

## 3. Working physical interpretation

The most defensible current interpretation is:

### 3.1 The bridge is structural, not pointwise
The relevant information does not appear to live in one isolated number, but in a configuration of relational quantities.

This is physically important because it aligns with the broader project logic:
- correlations are primary,
- geometry is emergent,
- structure matters more than coordinate-level representation.

### 3.2 The bridge is a regime transition, not a rigid identity
The current evidence does not support the idea that FSW and AO are simply the same object in different notation.

Instead, the evidence suggests:
- partial structural overlap,
- stable transition relations,
- and a common transitional order without full identity.

This makes the bridge physically richer: it looks like a **contact zone** or **translation zone** between regimes, not a trivial equivalence.

### 3.3 The bridge is multi-component
The H2 reduced-mode findings are especially informative here.

The H2 holdout:
- remained weak rather than failed,
- retained strong separation,
- retained strong assignment,
- retained CI-based stability components,
- but lost the full inherited Type-B-pattern gate.

This suggests that the bridge remains partially visible under incomplete representation, but its full internal identity is not triggered unless the relevant structure is represented in sufficient completeness.

That is exactly what one would expect from a genuinely multi-component physical bridge.

## 4. Candidate bridge carriers vs diagnostic scaffolding

The current project state allows a first provisional distinction.

## 4.1 Likely primary bridge carriers
These quantities currently appear closest to the physical structure of the transition itself:

- `distance_to_type_D`
- `spacing_cv`
- `simple_rigidity_surrogate`
- `grid_deviation_score`

### Why these appear primary
They do not merely score success or failure of the pipeline.  
They describe where a case lies in the structural space of the transition:

- distance from a reference structural type,
- regularity vs deviation in spacing structure,
- rigidity-like resistance or structural stiffness,
- departure from smooth grid-like ordering.

Taken together, they describe the **shape of the transitional regime**, not just whether a classifier likes it.

---

## 4.2 Likely secondary or diagnostic quantities
These quantities are methodologically important, but currently appear to function more as **evaluation scaffolding** than as physical bridge carriers:

- `type_B_like_pattern_detected`
- `original_vs_control_margin_delta`
- `original_stability_score`
- `assignment_score`
- `holdout result labels` such as `supported`, `weak`, `failed`
- summary pass/fail thresholds
- ranking and holdout decision logic

### Why these appear secondary
They are primarily useful for:
- robustness assessment,
- exclusion logic,
- comparative evaluation,
- and publication-grade defensibility.

They tell us whether a physical reading is robust enough to trust, but they are not themselves the physical content of the bridge.

## 5. Provisional geometry of the bridge in feature space

The current findings support a provisional geometric picture.

## 5.1 FSW as a quantitative corridor
FSW currently looks like the more corridor-like regime:
- more regular,
- more constrained,
- more quantitative in how the transition is organized.

In this picture, FSW is not the whole bridge, but a relatively narrow and orderly segment of it.

## 5.2 AO as a structural extension / jump zone
AO appears to extend beyond the FSW corridor:
- especially in stronger or jump-like regions,
- with more pronounced structural deviation,
- and potentially with dynamics that FSW does not fully reproduce.

This suggests that AO is not merely “FSW plus noise,” but may represent an expanded continuation of the bridge into a richer transition sector.

## 5.3 Type-B as shared transitional order
The current Type-B reading is therefore best treated as:
- a shared transitional order,
- not a rigid identity class,
- and not a single regime.

Type-B appears to mark a region where relational order becomes transferable across descriptions, even when the full internal pattern identity is not preserved.

## 5.4 H2 as transport test of the bridge
The first H2 holdout provides a first transport test.

It does not show that the full bridge has already been reconstructed externally.  
But it does show that the signal is not confined entirely to the original local setup.

This is physically significant:
- the bridge is not purely local to one internal calibration context,
- but its full pattern identity is fragile under reduced transfer.

That is consistent with a real bridge that is:
- partially transportable,
- but not trivially universal.

## 6. What the current evidence already supports

The following statements are already reasonably supported:

1. The bridge is not best modeled as a single-marker phenomenon.
2. The bridge is more plausibly a relational transition structure.
3. FSW and AO are not simply identical, but they are not disconnected either.
4. The bridge appears to require multiple jointly present structural quantities.
5. Transfer preserves a meaningful part of the signal even when the full-pattern gate is lost.

These are already nontrivial physical constraints.

## 7. What remains physically open

Several key questions remain open and define the next phase.

## 7.1 Necessity vs support
Which candidate bridge carriers are:
- necessary,
- sufficient,
- or only supportive?

This is currently the most important open physical discrimination task.

## 7.2 Bridge geometry
What is the bridge geometrically in feature space?

Is it best understood as:
- a corridor,
- a layered transition shell,
- a branching fan,
- a path bundle,
- or a jump-enabled contact manifold?

At present, the evidence is consistent with a corridor-plus-extension picture, but this remains provisional.

## 7.3 Proxy sensitivity
The H2 reduced-mode results suggest that one missing direct feature can leave the signal visible while blocking the full pattern identity.

This raises a major physical question:
- which quantities are genuinely irreplaceable,
- and which can be approximated without destroying the bridge?

## 7.4 Dynamic meaning
A further open question is whether the bridge is purely structural, or already encodes something dynamically deeper:
- a stability principle,
- a transition law,
- or a precursor of emergent geometric order.

That question is not yet answered, but the current findings make it legitimate to ask.

## 8. Recommended next physical tasks

The following next steps follow naturally from the present synthesis.

### 8.1 Carrier reduction experiment
Systematically remove or replace candidate bridge variables and test when the bridge picture collapses.

Goal:
- identify necessary vs merely supportive quantities.

### 8.2 Bridge geometry visualization
Construct 2D / 3D views of the relevant variables, with emphasis not only on clusters but on:
- paths,
- curvature,
- bottlenecks,
- extensions,
- and jump regions.

Goal:
- make the bridge visible as a geometric transition object.

### 8.3 AO-overextension analysis
Study more explicitly where AO departs from the FSW corridor and whether those departures correspond to:
- noise,
- extension,
- new sector,
- or genuine dynamical enrichment.

### 8.4 Literature comparison as context, not constraint
Compare the emerging bridge variables with established literature only after the internal structure has been clarified.

Goal:
- find alignment without forcing the project’s own bridge into foreign categories too early.
