# PRE_READABLE_TYPE_B_FIELD_OF_VIEW_NOTE

## Field of view for the first pre-readable Type-B subset selection

**Date:** 2026-04-07  
**Status:** internal field-of-view note

---

## 1. Purpose
This note defines the broad internal Type-B field of view within which the first pre-readable subset selection should take place.

Its purpose is to answer one practical question:

> **What is the actual Type-B map on which the first pre-readable pass should perform its no-go filtering, candidate-subset ranking, and final subset choice?**

This note does not yet choose the final subset.
It defines the selection terrain.

This is a field-of-view note, not a result note.

---

## 2. Why a field of view is needed
The pre-readable Type-B block is now methodically far enough that subset selection cannot begin from an undefined background.

The block already has:

- an organization-side marker:
  - **A1 = structured support coherence**
- a readability-side boundary:
  - **`grid_deviation_score`**
- a threshold rule
- a comparison rule
- a Type-B transitional-window binding
- a segment-selection rule
- and a selection-record template

But all of these still need one higher-level frame:

- **the field of view**

Without that, the first subset selection remains too exposed to:
- ad hoc map choice
- shifting terrain assumptions
- and hidden redefinition of what counts as “inside Type-B”

So the field of view must now be fixed explicitly.

---

## 3. Core field-of-view principle
The field of view should obey one central principle:

> **Include the internal Type-B terrain in which bridge-relevant organization may plausibly gather and transition toward readability, but exclude terrain that belongs to clearly different methodological roles or lies outside the bridge-relevant internal Type-B landscape.**

A shorter version is:

> **Use the internal Type-B bridge terrain, not the whole world around it.**

That is the selection principle.

---

## 4. What the field of view is supposed to contain
The first pre-readable Type-B field of view should contain:

- internal Type-B bridge-relevant regions
- internal transition terrain between weak organization and later readability-side formation
- bridge-adjacent regions in which support-side organization may plausibly begin to gather
- local or mesoscopic zones that could later be narrowed into candidate subsets

This field of view should therefore include:
- more than one possible subset candidate
but
- less than the whole global project landscape

That is the intended scale.

---

## 5. What the field of view is not supposed to contain
The field of view should **not** contain terrain whose role is already better handled by other methodological blocks or is outside the relevant internal Type-B bridge landscape.

This should exclude in particular:

### 5.1 Ring / boundary-case terrain
The ring block is a distinct boundary-case stress-test line and should not be mixed into the first pre-readable Type-B selection map.

### 5.2 Non-Type-B external background
Terrain outside the internal Type-B bridge-relevant space should not be allowed to influence first-pass subset choice.

### 5.3 Fully unrelated scaffold or infrastructure space
Administrative, scaffold, or unrelated methodological spaces are not part of the field of view.

### 5.4 Terrain already dedicated to clearly different comparison logic
If a region belongs primarily to another methodological problem, it should not be smuggled into the pre-readable Type-B map by convenience.

These exclusions keep the map clean.

---

## 6. Recommended field-of-view definition
The first field of view should therefore be defined as:

> **the internal Type-B bridge-relevant terrain spanning weakly organized bridge-adjacent regions, transitional organization zones, and late-near but not yet pre-excluded readability-adjacent internal regions, while excluding ring terrain, non-Type-B external background, and unrelated methodological space**

This is the preferred full field-of-view definition.

A shorter version is:

> **the internal Type-B bridge terrain from weak organization toward readability, before subset-level filtering**

That is the intended map.

---

## 7. Why the field of view must be broader than the final subset
The field of view must not be confused with the final target subset.

The field of view should be:
- broad enough to allow filtering and ranking

The final subset should be:
- narrow enough to act as the actual first-pass case

If the field of view is too narrow:
- the selection becomes circular
- the final subset is effectively pre-chosen

If the field of view is too broad:
- the map loses interpretability
- and multiple unrelated terrains get mixed together

So the field of view should be:
- broad but still internal and relevant

That is the correct balance.

---

## 8. Relationship to the no-go filter
The field-of-view note comes before the no-go filter.

This means:

- the field of view defines the terrain to be searched
- the no-go filter removes weak background, clearly formed readability-side zones, and exotic oddities inside that terrain
- and only after that does candidate subset ranking begin

This order must remain explicit.

The field of view is the map.
The no-go filter is the first cut on the map.

---

## 9. Relationship to the final bridge-adjacent mesoscopic transition subset
The final subset will later be selected **inside** this field of view.

That means the field-of-view note should already support the later logic by ensuring that the map contains:

- enough weak-to-strong internal variation
- enough bridge-adjacent structure
- enough transition terrain
- and enough readability-near but not already pre-excluded variation

Only then can the later selection workflow work properly.

So the field of view is not a background convenience.
It is a structural prerequisite.

---

## 10. Why a field of view is especially important for the pre-readable block
The pre-readable block is unusually vulnerable to narrative drift because the key distinction is layered and subtle:

- organization already present
- readability not yet formed

If the field of view is not fixed, one can too easily:
- move the map
- choose an easier region later
- or silently avoid the genuinely transitional terrain

That would weaken the whole block.

So field-of-view discipline is especially important here.

---

## 11. Recommended practical reading
A useful practical reading is:

- first define the internal Type-B bridge terrain broadly
- then remove the obvious no-go zones
- then identify candidate bridge-adjacent mesoscopic transition subsets
- then rank and choose

This should be the standard reading order.

A shorter operational phrase is:

> **Map first, filter second, subset third.**

This is the right practical logic.

---

## 12. Safe summary sentence
A useful internal sentence is:

> The first pre-readable pass should begin from the internal Type-B bridge-relevant terrain as its field of view, rather than from a pre-selected subset or from the broader project landscape.

A shorter version is:

> **First define the internal Type-B map; only then choose the subset.**

Both are good steering lines.

---

## 13. Bottom line
The field of view for the first pre-readable Type-B subset selection should now be fixed as:

> **the internal Type-B bridge-relevant terrain spanning weakly organized bridge-adjacent regions, transitional organization zones, and readability-near internal regions before subset-level filtering**

This field of view excludes:
- ring terrain
- non-Type-B external background
- and unrelated methodological space

That is the current field-of-view standard for the first pre-readable Type-B pass.
