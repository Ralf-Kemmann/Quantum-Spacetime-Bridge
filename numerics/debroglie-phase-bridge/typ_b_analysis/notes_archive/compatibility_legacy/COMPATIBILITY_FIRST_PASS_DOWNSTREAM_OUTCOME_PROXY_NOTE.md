# COMPATIBILITY_FIRST_PASS_DOWNSTREAM_OUTCOME_PROXY_NOTE

## Downstream outcome proxy for the first compatibility-candidate pass

**Date:** 2026-04-07  
**Status:** internal outcome-proxy note

---

## 1. Purpose
This note defines the role and minimum requirements of the downstream outcome proxy used in the first compatibility-candidate pass.

Its purpose is to answer one necessary question:

> **If Candidate A and Candidate B are earlier-than-stabilization signals, what later-but-still-narrow outcome should they be judged against in the first pass?**

This note is needed because the first pass is only coherent if all three positions are fixed:

- lower floor: overlap-only baseline
- upper boundary: later stabilization boundary
- downstream target: one narrow later outcome proxy

This is an outcome-proxy note, not a result note.

---

## 2. Why a downstream outcome proxy is needed
The first compatibility pass is not just asking whether candidates “look interesting.”

It is asking whether they provide meaningful value relative to a later outcome.

Without an explicit downstream proxy, the first pass becomes too soft:
- candidates can look promising without predicting anything specific
- different readers may imagine different outcomes
- and weak structure can be overread because the target was never fixed

So the downstream proxy is required to close the comparison logic.

---

## 3. Intended role of the downstream outcome proxy
The downstream outcome proxy should function as:

- a later target than the compatibility candidates,
- but a narrower target than full readability, morphology, or ontology,
- and a target interpretable enough that the first pass remains disciplined.

So the proxy is not the whole bridge theory.
It is only the first narrow downstream comparison target.

That is its correct role.

---

## 4. What the proxy must do
The downstream outcome proxy must satisfy all of the following conditions.

## 4.1 Later than Candidate A and Candidate B
It must sit downstream of the compatibility candidates.

Otherwise it is not a later outcome and cannot serve the first-pass purpose.

---

## 4.2 Narrower than full readability
It must remain much narrower than:
- full regime readability
- later morphology
- broad geometry claims
- or any large ontological interpretation

The first pass is not ready for targets that broad.

---

## 4.3 Interpretable
It must be clear what success or failure on the proxy means.

The proxy should not be so abstract that every movement can be narrated as “interesting.”

---

## 4.4 Stable enough to compare against
It should be specific enough that:
- Candidate A
- Candidate B
- overlap-only baseline
- and the later stabilization boundary

can all be discussed relative to the same downstream target.

---

## 5. What the proxy must not be
The downstream outcome proxy must **not** be:

### 5.1 The whole bridge mechanism
It is not allowed to stand in for:
- complete mechanism closure
- complete bridge explanation
- or compatibility solved

### 5.2 Full readability or morphology
It should not simply be:
- `grid_deviation_score`
- late morphology
- or broad readable structure

That would be too far downstream and too broad for the first pass.

### 5.3 The same thing as the stabilization boundary
It should not simply be identical to:
- `simple_rigidity_surrogate`

because then the candidate would be tested against the same thing that also defines the upper boundary.

The boundary and the target must remain conceptually distinct.

### 5.4 A vague “interestingness” score
The proxy must be explicit and narrow enough to prevent interpretive drift.

---

## 6. Working conceptual definition
The current best conceptual definition is:

> **The first-pass downstream outcome proxy should be a narrow later bridge-support-related indicator that is later than the candidate signals, but still narrower and cleaner than broad readability-side or ontology-side outcomes.**

A shorter version is:

> **Later than compatibility, narrower than full bridge readability.**

That is the intended position.

---

## 7. Recommended proxy class
The recommended proxy class for the first pass is:

- **narrow later bridge-support / support-response indicator**

This means the proxy should be:
- support-related
- later than Candidate A and B
- but still specific enough to remain usable in a first-pass setting

Examples of the right class:
- a narrow later support-success indicator
- a local or mesoscopic bridge-support response measure
- a limited persistence-related downstream marker
- a later support classification narrower than full morphology/readability

This is the right family of targets.

---

## 8. Why a narrow support proxy is better than a broad readability target
A narrow support proxy is better because it keeps the first pass close to the actual mechanism gap.

The compatibility layer is supposed to sit between:
- overlap
and
- stabilization

So the first later target should stay close to:
- later support behavior

not jump too quickly to:
- large morphology
- full readability
- or high-level bridge interpretation

A broad target would overinflate the first pass.

---

## 9. Relationship to the overlap baseline
The downstream proxy must be later than the overlap-only baseline.

This matters because the first pass asks:
- does Candidate A or B provide more than raw relational presence?

So the downstream proxy must be later enough that raw overlap alone does not already trivialize the entire comparison.

---

## 10. Relationship to the later-stabilization boundary
The downstream outcome proxy must remain conceptually distinct from the later-stabilization boundary.

The distinction should be:

- **stabilization boundary** = upper anti-tautology ceiling
- **downstream outcome proxy** = later comparison target

These two may be related, but they must not be treated as identical.

If the same scalar serves both roles without distinction, the first pass becomes muddy.

---

## 11. Working first-pass recommendation
For the first pass, the downstream proxy should be fixed as:

> **one narrow later bridge-support-related outcome indicator, defined explicitly and kept constant for both Candidate A and Candidate B**

This should be chosen before candidate comparison begins.

It should then be:
- fixed
- named
- and not redefined mid-pass

This is mandatory.

---

## 12. What would count as a good first-pass downstream proxy
A good first-pass downstream proxy would have the following properties:

- later than Candidate A and Candidate B
- not identical to the overlap baseline
- not identical to the rigidity-side boundary
- narrow enough for disciplined comparison
- interpretable enough that candidate improvements mean something specific

That is the ideal profile.

---

## 13. Main risks of a bad outcome proxy
If the downstream proxy is chosen badly, the entire first pass weakens.

### Main risks
- proxy too broad
- proxy too close to full readability
- proxy identical to the stabilization boundary
- proxy too vague to interpret
- proxy redefined after candidates have already been discussed

These must all be prevented.

---

## 14. Required first-pass discipline
For the first pass, the downstream outcome proxy must be:

- fixed before candidate comparison begins
- identical for Candidate A and Candidate B
- recorded explicitly in the first-pass result
- narrow enough to stay operationally useful
- and not broadened mid-pass

This is mandatory.

---

## 15. Safe summary sentence
A useful internal sentence is:

> The first compatibility pass needs a downstream target that is later than the candidate signals but still much narrower than full bridge readability, so that the comparison remains mechanistically meaningful rather than interpretively inflated.

A shorter version is:

> **Later than A and B, narrower than full readability.**

Both are good steering sentences.

---

## 16. Bottom line
The first-pass downstream outcome proxy is the final comparison target needed to close the first compatibility-candidate block.

It should be:

- later than Candidate A and Candidate B
- narrower than full readability or morphology
- distinct from the overlap baseline
- distinct from the rigidity-side boundary
- explicit, fixed, and interpretable

That is the current downstream-outcome-proxy standard for the first compatibility pass.
