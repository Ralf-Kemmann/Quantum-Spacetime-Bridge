# COMPATIBILITY_FIRST_PASS_CANDIDATE_PAIR_NOTE

## Why Candidate A and Candidate B form the correct first-pass pair

**Date:** 2026-04-07  
**Status:** internal pairing note

---

## 1. Purpose
This note defines why the first compatibility-layer comparison pass should begin with exactly the following candidate pair:

- **Candidate A:** local support coherence
- **Candidate B:** early removal fragility

Its purpose is to prevent the first pass from looking like an arbitrary two-candidate selection.

Instead, this note makes explicit:

- why these two candidates belong together,
- what distinct roles they play,
- what they jointly test,
- and what they are not yet supposed to prove.

This is a pairing note, not a result note.

---

## 2. Why a pairing note is needed
The compatibility-layer work is now methodologically structured enough that the first pass should not begin with loose candidate accumulation.

At this stage, discipline matters more than variety.

The project already has:
- a minimal compatibility pilot frame,
- a candidate evaluation rule,
- a candidate priority structure,
- a baseline-comparison rule,
- a first-pass workflow,
- and now two defined Tier-1 candidate notes.

What is still needed is a clear statement of why these two candidates are the correct first-pass pair.

This note provides that statement.

---

## 3. The central pairing logic
The central logic is simple:

> **Candidate A and Candidate B are not two arbitrary candidates. They are two complementary Tier-1 windows onto the same missing layer between overlap and stabilization.**

They ask different questions about the same gap.

- **Candidate A** asks whether a local relation environment already looks internally coherent enough to count as early supportability.
- **Candidate B** asks whether a local relation already behaves as structurally support-critical under controlled weakening before full stabilization is taken for granted.

So the pair jointly probes:
- coherence-side supportability
and
- fragility-side supportability

This makes them a good first-pass pair.

---

## 4. Role of Candidate A in the pair

### 4.1 Candidate A as the coherence-side window
Candidate A is the more “positive” or constructive side of the pair.

Its guiding question is:
- where does the local environment already hang together in a way that looks more structured than generic overlap?

This candidate is useful because compatibility may plausibly begin as:
- local mutual fit,
- local agreement,
- local reduction of internal conflict,
- or a local environment that is already able to support a common mode in principle.

### 4.2 What Candidate A is meant to reveal
Candidate A is meant to reveal:
- whether compatibility first becomes visible as local internal supportability

This is the coherence-side interpretation of the missing layer.

---

## 5. Role of Candidate B in the pair

### 5.1 Candidate B as the fragility-side window
Candidate B is the more “stress-response” side of the pair.

Its guiding question is:
- which local relations reveal disproportionate structural importance when weakened or removed early enough?

This candidate is useful because compatibility may plausibly also show up as:
- hidden dependence,
- local support criticality,
- or structural vulnerability that generic overlap does not explain.

### 5.2 What Candidate B is meant to reveal
Candidate B is meant to reveal:
- whether compatibility first becomes visible as early support fragility rather than local agreement

This is the fragility-side interpretation of the missing layer.

---

## 6. Why these two candidates complement each other
The pair is strong because the two candidates approach the same open mechanism from opposite but compatible sides.

### Candidate A emphasizes
- local fit
- local consistency
- supportability by agreement

### Candidate B emphasizes
- local dependence
- structural sensitivity
- supportability by fragility under perturbation

These are not redundant.
They are complementary.

That is exactly why they belong together in the first pass.

---

## 7. Why this pair is better than a wider first pass
A wider first pass would currently be methodologically weaker.

For example, adding immediately:
- sign/sector contrast
- structured asymmetry
- morphology-near candidates
- stability-near candidates

would create too much spread and too much rescue room.

The A+B pair is better because it is:

- small
- Tier-1 on both sides
- conceptually distinct
- and still narrow enough for disciplined rejection if needed

That is exactly what a first pass should be.

---

## 8. What the pair jointly tests
The pair does **not** test whether compatibility is already solved.

It tests only this:

> **Does the missing layer between overlap and stabilization begin to show itself more plausibly as local support coherence, as early removal fragility, or as neither?**

That is the correct joint test question.

This is already enough for a valuable first pass.

---

## 9. What the pair does not yet do
This pair does not yet justify:

- mechanism closure
- compatibility-layer isolation
- a final metric family
- broader bridge-theory completion
- or any external strong claim

At most, this pair can show:
- one candidate fails,
- both fail,
- one survives weakly,
- or one becomes promising enough for second-pass refinement

Nothing stronger.

---

## 10. Pair-specific risks
The pair is useful, but not risk-free.

### 10.1 Risk on Candidate A side
Candidate A may collapse into:
- smoothed overlap
- or weak local density

### 10.2 Risk on Candidate B side
Candidate B may collapse into:
- late-stage stabilization leakage
- or a disguised survival-damage analysis

### 10.3 Pair-level risk
The pair could fail not because compatibility is absent, but because the first two Tier-1 operationalizations are still too weak or too early-stage.

So pair failure must be interpreted carefully.

---

## 11. Why pair failure would still be useful
Even if both candidates fail, the pair is still scientifically valuable.

Why?
Because the first pass would then establish something important:

- the compatibility layer remains open
- neither local agreement nor early fragility was enough in its current form
- the gap is real and not easily filled by the most obvious Tier-1 candidate families

That is still progress.

So the pair is worthwhile even under failure.

---

## 12. Why pair success would be useful
If one candidate survives or looks promising, the gain is also clear:

- the compatibility layer would begin to become operationally visible
- the project would gain a second-pass focus
- and the missing mechanism gap would become narrower and more structured

That would be a major step.

So the pair is high-value under both:
- failure
- and partial success

This is another reason it is the correct first-pass pair.

---

## 13. Recommended interpretation discipline for the pair
The pair should be interpreted under a strict sequence:

1. Can A survive the anti-overlap / anti-tautology logic?
2. Can B survive the anti-overlap / anti-tautology logic?
3. Only then ask which one is stronger
4. Only then decide whether anything deserves second-pass refinement

This order matters.

The first question is not:
- which candidate is better?

The first question is:
- does either candidate survive at all?

That is the correct discipline.

---

## 14. Safe summary sentence
A useful summary sentence is:

> Candidate A and Candidate B form the correct first-pass compatibility pair because they probe the same missing layer from two complementary Tier-1 directions: local support coherence and early structural fragility.

A shorter version is:

> **A asks where support already hangs together; B asks where support already breaks disproportionately.**

Both are good internal steering lines.

---

## 15. Bottom line
The first compatibility pass should begin with exactly this pair:

- **Candidate A = local support coherence**
- **Candidate B = early removal fragility**

This pair is strong because it is:
- narrow,
- complementary,
- Tier-1 on both sides,
- and well aligned with the current mechanism gap between overlap and stabilization.

It does not solve compatibility.
But it is the right first pair for asking whether compatibility begins to become operationally visible at all.
